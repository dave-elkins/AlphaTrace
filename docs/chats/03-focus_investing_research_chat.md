The core premise is that if you are a skilled stock picker, diversifying too broadly dilutes your "best ideas" and guarantees mediocre results. Here is a breakdown of the key research and findings in this field.

### 1. The "Hagstrom" Simulations (1999)

Robert Hagstrom's book, *The Warren Buffett Portfolio*, contains some of the most famous research on this topic. He used Monte Carlo simulations to compare the performance of 3,000 randomly generated portfolios.

- **Finding:** Portfolios with only 15 to 25 stocks had a significantly higher probability of beating the S&P 500 compared to portfolios with 100+ stocks.
- **Trade-off:** While the upside was higher, the volatility (standard deviation) was also much higher. Hagstrom concluded that focus investing requires a "stomach for volatility" that most retail investors lack.

### 2. Academic Support: Active Share and Outperformance

Modern academic research often uses the metric "Active Share" to measure how much a portfolio differs from its benchmark.

- **Cremers and Petajisto (2009):** Their landmark paper, "How Active is Your Fund Manager?", found that managers with high Active Share (those who take concentrated bets away from the index) tend to outperform their benchmarks, even after fees.
- **Kacperczyk, Sialm, and Zheng (2005):** Their research showed that mutual fund managers who "focus" their portfolios in industries where they have a distinct information advantage tend to deliver higher risk-adjusted returns.
- **Evans and Archer (1968):** This classic study is often cited by focus investors. It suggested that as few as 10 to 15 stocks provide the majority of the benefits of diversification. Beyond that, the marginal reduction in risk is minimal compared to the potential loss in return.

### 3. Recent Corporate Research (BCG 2025)

A recent study by the Boston Consulting Group (BCG) in 2025 highlighted that "focus beats diversification" not just for investors, but for companies themselves. They found that companies with focused business portfolios (concentrating resources on a few core segments) outperformed their more "conglomerated" or diversified peers in total shareholder return (TSR).

### 4. Comparison: Focused vs. Diversified

### 5. The "Munger" Philosophy

Charlie Munger was perhaps the most vocal critic of "over-diversification," which he famously called "deworsification." His research (and practice) suggested that:

- **Bet Heavily on Rare Odds:** When the market misprices a high-quality company, you should bet a large percentage of your capital.
- **Circle of Competence:** Focus only on what you truly understand. Diversification is a "hedge against ignorance"—if you don't know what you're doing, you must diversify. If you do know, it's a mistake.

**The "Catch":** Research consistently highlights one major risk: The "Fat Tail" Problem. In a focused portfolio, a single "permanent loss of capital" (a company going to zero) can be catastrophic. Therefore, focused investing is only supported by research when combined with rigorous fundamental analysis and a long-term time horizon.
